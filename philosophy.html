You don't have 300 tasks. You have one job.

And after that one, another one. Here's the whole idea the app is built to protect.

The list is the enemy

Every task app shows you the list, and the list radiates guilt in proportion to its length. Forty-seven open items is not information — it's dread, delivered on schedule.

One Job shows you one card. The thing in front of you, full-screen, nothing else clamoring. Everything about the app is a consequence of defending that single decision — at every scale, forever.

Write one song

The musician Jeff Tweedy wrote a book called How to Write One Song. His point: "write songs" is an abstract, anxious, quality-haunted goal, and it freezes you. "Write one song" is concrete — that song has its own qualities and leads you through its own making.

Tasks work the same way. "Be productive" is a goal that generates nothing but pressure. Do this one card is something you can actually do — and then it's done, and there's another one, and that's the whole rhythm of a day.

A pack of cards, not a screen

The phone in your hand isn't a screen showing you a card — it is the card. Text fills it. Swipe it away and the next one is waiting. Any card can hold a small deck of its own, so a big task becomes a few small cards you play the same way, as deep as the work really goes.

We love the physical feel of this, but not literally — a real deck of cards doesn't grow 700 inches long. Ours is felt physicality: a pile that visibly thickens as it grows and settles into "wow, that's a big deck," never a number. Cards left untended long enough start to yellow at the edges — the passing of time as a bit of whimsy, not a warning.

Two gestures carry everything

No menus to learn. The whole system is two directions, and where you are supplies the meaning.

Swipe right →

Done. Move it along.

← Swipe left

Not yet — to the bottom of the deck, back around when you're ready.

Finished cards don't vanish into a void. They move through Done, Archive, and Trash — and every step can be walked back. Change your mind, restore a card, send it home to your deck. Deleting forever takes a deliberate, confirmed step. Nothing else is a dead end.

Fun, not dread

Capturing a task and looking at it on a card should be a small pleasure. You'll notice when you keep putting something off — that noticing is useful — but it should feel like a wry observation, not an accusation.

No red badges counting your failures. No "you're behind." No missed- deadline guilt trip. The app that helps you focus shouldn't be another thing making you anxious.

It's yours, and it stays yours

One Job runs on your device. No account to create, nothing tracking your day, no server holding your tasks hostage. It works offline. Your whole deck exports to a plain file you can keep anywhere — and the app only ever tells you a backup succeeded when it actually watched it happen.

An update should never cost you your data. That one isn't a feature. It's a promise.

See one task. Do one task. Feel accomplished.
